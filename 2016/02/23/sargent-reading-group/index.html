Pau Roldan

The Financing Of Ideas And The Great Deviation by Garcia-Macia (2015)

Facts:

  • only crisis in recent history without trend reversion. Author claims key reason is that it was a financial crisis.
  • Frictions are worse for intangible capital than tangible capital

Two assumptions:

  1. intangible capital is harder to seized by investors in case of default
  2. intangible capital faces a higher depreciation rate.

Model:

  • SOE.
  • production
    • continuum of firms, output aggregated in CES
    • firms use labor, physical cap, intangible cap. Increasing returns
    • Fixed cost every period
  • Capital
    • Convex adj. cost.
    • mean reverting shocks to depreciation of intangible capital
  • Financing:
    • dividend
    • 1 period default-able debt (in either type of capital). If default, debt-holders only recover fraction of principal. This fraction lower for intangible capital
  • lots of shocks, more stuff, …

Equilibrium:

  • Firms with low depreciation shock will expect depreciation to stay low –> more intangible capital intensive
  • In financial crisis intangible capital hit harder, so firms with low depreciation shocks hit harder

Jonathan Payne

Managing A Liquidity Trap: Monetary And Fiscal Policy by Ivan Werning (2012)

Model:

  • perfect foresight
  • continuous time
  • log-linear NK model (phillips and IS curves)

Experiment: natural interest rate will be negative for a while, then will jump up and be positive.

Planner problem: choose interest rate in LQ problem (squares of output gap, spending rate, government spending gap)

Is able to do 4 experiments in closed form because he has a simple model and perfect foresight.

Josh Weiss

Moral Hazard Versus Liquidity And Optimal Unemployment Insurance by Chetty (2008)

Main purpose: should insurance be higher or lower (not looking for optimal level)?

Model:

  • discrete time, finite horizon, no discounting, no borrowing
  • searches every period for constant cost. Once matches, keeps job at constant wage forever
  • government has balanced BC, that taxes labor income to finance unemployment

Equilibrium:

  • Impact of increased unemployment benefits
    • liquidity effect: can’t borrow while searching – search more
    • wage lower when get job b/c gov BC must be balanced – search less
  • Only uses model to produce a welfare function he can use in data to analyze two effects above

Igor Cesarec

Downward Nominal Wage Rigidity, Currency Pegs, And Involuntary Unemployment by Schmitt-grohe And Uribe (2016)

Model:

  • two goods: tradable and non tradable
  • HH supply labor inelastically
  • internationally traded bond, natural borrowing limit, exog stoch interest rate
  • Firms
    • use labor Only
    • stochastic endowment of tradable goods
  • Nominal wage downwardly Rigidity
    • Wage can’t fall more than gamma times previous wage. Can rise infinitely (use Argentine data to motivate) -

Analysis in currency peg model: - Two nominal rigidities (fixed exchange rate, rigid wage) - Lead rigidity in real wage (nominal/interest rate – rigidity in top and bottom) - Could be avoided by dropping exchange rate by devaluing currency. Can’t in pegged model. - Can’t change the r, need to change w. Can do in with fiscal policy – just tax wage to make it fall. Hard to implement – people don’t like tax - Instead they explore optimal taxes on foreign borrowing (capital controls). Solve with Ramsey problem. - Leads to smoothing consumption by smoothing out wage path (goes up less in booms and down less in busts, relative to model w/out capital controls) - Problem is distorting intertemporal margins. Plus is lower unemployment in recessions (also mean and volatility of unemp both lower).

Miguel Faria E Castro

Regional Redistribution Through The U.S. Mortgage Market by Hurst, Keys, Seru, Vavra(2015)

Facts about how mortgages work in US:

  • Private securities price default risk. GSE (goverment issued, fannie mae and freedie mac) doesn’t, so they act as a resdistribution vehicle across default risk
  • After controlling for borrower characteristics no relationship between delinquincy rate and GSE mortgages, but a strong relationship between the delinquincy rate and private mortgages.
  • Build a model to look at what would happen to welfare if they shut down these redistribution mechanisms. Build off a Bewley model

Pau Roldan

The Growth Dynamics Of Innovation, Diffusion, And The Technology Frontier by Benhabib, Perla And Tonetti (2015)

  • Extension of model where we create growth through diffusion of technology (Perla Tonetti)
  • Result of previous paper is that there is a threshold above which all firms imitate or act like the most productive firms.
    • Given Frechet initial condition, there is a unique stationary distr.
    • Main problem is that support of stationary distr is unbounded.
  • Interaction between innovation and imitation that permits a stationary distribution of productivity
  • Key mechanism here is that some firms find it optimal to not innovate because other firms around them get a higher return on innovation and they can simply imitate.
  • In this paper if you allow for leapfrogging (some firms moving right to frontier) the stationary distribution is unique and bounded.

Jonathan Payne

Constrained Efficiency In The Neoclassical Growth Model With Uninsurable Idiosyncratic Shocks by Julio Davila, Jay H Hong, Per Krusell (2012)

  • Estimating welfare in Bewley/Huggett/Ayigari models.
  • Think about constrained pareto optimality as defined by «could planner do better than CE when he has to obey constraints». Only difference planner has is that he internalizes impact of his choices on interest rates/prices.
  • Planner is utilitarian (equal weights on all agents).

Josh Weiss

Risk Taking By Entrepreneurs by Vereshchagina And Hopenhayn (2009)

  • Entrepreneurs tend to take on more risk and learn lower returns than other investors. So why do they do it?
  • This paper writes a simple/short model that tries to answer this question.
  • You can switch occupations from being a worker to an Entrepreneurs. Once entrepreneur always an entrepreneur
  • Entrepreneurs have access to high return technology.
  • Worker VF has kink when you decide to become Entrepreneur. Leads to non-concavity in VF
  • If you are in this local non-concavity region of wealth, you can randomize over being each type of agent and this effectively makes the VF concave.
