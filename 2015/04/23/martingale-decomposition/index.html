Martingale Decomposition of SDF

Notes on identifying martingale component of SDF

Misspecified Recovery (Hansen, Borovicka, Scheinkman; 2014)

Framework Notation

  • $\left{ \Omega, \mathcal{F}, P \right}$: probability space
  • $T$: set of time indices
  • $X = \left{X_t : t \in T \right}$: stationary n-d Markov process
  • $W$: jointly (with $X$) stationary stochastic process
  • $\mathcal{F} = \left{ \mathcal{F}_t: t \in T\right}$: Filtration generated by $\left{ W\right}$ and $X_0$

In continuous time we write the evolution of $X$ and the log of a multiplicative functional as

\begin{align} dX_t = \mu(X_t) dt + \sigma(X_t) dW_t \ d \log M_t &= \beta(X_t) dt + \alpha(X_t) \cdot dW_t \end{align}

We will use notation $Y(X, W)$ to denote first-order Markov processes. The composite system $Y$ «has a triangular structure because $Xt$ is the relevant state vector for $(X{t+1}, \Delta W{t+1}$)«. In our continuous time setting this means that the distribution of $(X{t+\tau}, W_{t+\tau} - W_t)$, conditioned on $\mathcal{F}_t$ is only dependent on $X_t$.

SDF

The stochastic discount factor is a positive multiplicative functional $S$ with finite first moment such that at any time $\tau$, any $\mathcal{F}_{t}$ claim $\phi_t, t > \tau$ is priced as

$$\Pi_{\tau, t}(\phi_t) = E \left[ \frac{St}{S{\tau}} \phit \bigm\vert \cal{F}{\tau} \right].$$

Thus, for bounded claims dependent only on our Markov state, the price is

$$Q_t \psi = E[S_t \psi(X_t) | X_0 = x]$$

Martingales and Measures

let $H$ be an $\mathcal{F}$ martingale where $E[H0] = 1$. We can define another probability measure $P^H$ such that if $A \in \mathcal{F}{\tau}$ for some $\tau > 0$,

$$P^H(A) = E[1A H{\tau}].$$

In our continuous time model, we have (TODO: don’t understand this, shouldn’t martingale have zero drift):

$$\beta(x) = - \frac{1}{2} \alpha(x) \cdot \alpha(x).$$

Under the change of measure $W_t$ has a drift given by $\alpha(X_t)$. $Y$ will remain a martingale, under new measure, but $W$ and $X$ may not. Also $X$ may not be stationary under $P^H$.

Identification

A SDF and probability measure $(S, P)$ explains asset prices for all adapted, bounded claims. If we let $H$ be a multiplicative functional and a martingale, then together with the probability measure $P^H$ we can define

$$S^H = S \frac{H_0}{H}.$$

Proposition: Let $H$ is a multiplicative martingale (written according to form above) and $E[H_0] = 1$; and $S$ is a multiplicative functional with $S_0=1$. If $(S, P)$ describes asset prices, so does $(S^H, P^H)$. Also $S^H_0 = 1$.

The proposition tells us that SDFs are only well-defined for a specific probability measure. When we change the probability measure, we must also change the SDF. We now have multiple ways to write asset prices:

$$\Pi_{\tau, t}(\phi_t) = E \left[ \frac{St}{S{\tau}} \phit \bigm\vert \cal{F}{\tau} \right] = E^H \left[ \frac{S^Ht}{S^H{\tau}} \phit \bigm\vert \cal{F}{\tau} \right],$$

where $E^H$ is the expectation operator under $P^H$. Now, investors typically cannot observe $S$ or $P$ directly, but instead observe prices. Given this single data source, we cannot uniquely identify the pair $(S, P)$. In other words «we cannot recover investor beliefs from the Arrow prices alone. To achieve identification of investor beliefs, either we have to restrict the stochastic discount factor process $S$ or we have to restrict the probability distribution used to represent the valuation operators $\Pi_{\tau, t}$.»

There are many ways people have tried to address this lack of identification:

  • Impose rational expectations, observe time series of prices, let LLN for stationary distributions determine probabilities. This lets us identify $S$.
  • We might impose risk-neutral pricing, which means that the SDF is locally predictable and thus identifiable.
  • A new restriction, proposed here, of the form

$$S_t = \exp(- \delta_t) \frac{m(X_t)}{m(X_0)}.$$

Recovery

As we just discussed, observing asset prices does not identify both $S$ and $P$. However, if we assume that $X$ is stationary and ergodic under $P^H$, then we do get a unique identification of $S$ and $P$, where $S$ satisfies the functional form above (ERGODIC + SDF as specific MF => UNIQUENESS).

Proposition 5.3 Suppose $(S, P)$ explains asset prices and $S$ satisfies $S_t = \exp(- \delta_t) \frac{m(X_t)}{m(X_0)}.$ Let $H$ be a positive multiplicative martingale such that $(S^H, P^H)$ also explain asset prices and $X$ is stationary and ergodic under $P^H$. If $S^H also satisfies functional form of $S$, then $H \equiv 1$.

The proposition tells us that with functional form restriction on SDF and ergodicity of $S^H$, that we can uniquely identify both $S^H$ and $P^H$ from asset prices.

Relaxing functional form

If we relax the functional form assumption, but retain ergodicity, then we will recover an SDF that differs from the actual SDF by a martingale (the mtg. is equal to 1 and hence not distorting if and only if the functional form restriction is imposed – meaning if functional form is different, there must be distortion).

Perron-Frobenius problem: To find a scalar $\tilde{\eta}$ and a function $\tilde{e} > 0$ such that for every $t \in T$

$$ (Q_t \tilde{e})(x) = \exp(\tilde{\eta} t) \tilde{e}(x).$$

The solution will necessarily satisfy

$$E[S_t \tilde{e}X_t) | \mathcal{F}t] = \exp[(t - \tau) \tilde{\eta}] S{\tau} \tilde{e}(X_{\tau})$$

for $t \ge \tau$. When the state space is finite, functions of $X$ are all vectors and the expectation operator is a matrix. In this case we are solving an eigen vector problem. Eigenvectors are unique only up to a scalar multiple. In general state space models we have to solve the associated eigen-function problem and we might end up with multiple solutions, each unique only up to a scalar multiple. When we do have a solution, we will define a process $\tilde{H}$ as

$$\frac{\tilde{H}_t}{\tilde{H}_0} = \exp(- \tilde{\eta} t) S_t \frac{\tilde{e}(X_t)}{\tilde{e}(X_0)}.$$

The process $\tilde{H}$ is an $\mathcal{F}$ martingale under $P$. This construction of $\tilde{H}$ may not be unique, but if we impose the restriction that $X$ is stationary and ergodic under the implied change of measure, then there can be at most one such $\tilde{H}$ (uniqueness, but not necessarily existence).

Recall that $\tilde{H} = 1$ if and only if we restrict the form of the SDF. This leads us to a proposition

Proposition 6.2: $S$ satisfies functional form restriction if and only if there exists a solution $\tilde{e} >0$ to the Perron-Frobenius problem and the associated $\tilde{\eta}% such that

$$\frac{\tilde{H}_t}{\tilde{H}_0} = \exp(- \tilde{\eta} t) S_t \frac{\tilde{e}(X_t)}{\tilde{e}(X_0)} \equiv 1.$$

This proposition means that if the Perron-Frobenius problem has a solution where $\tilde{H} =1$, then the only measure that can be recovered is $P$. However, if $S$ contains a martingale component $\tilde{H} \ne 1$ (generally true) then $\tilde{P} \ne P$ is the measure that is recovered by solving the Perron-Frobenius problem. In other words, without the restriction on the functional form of $S$, the recovery procedure will not identify the actual probability measure, but will instead recover $\tilde{P}$ implied by $\tilde{H}$. The associated SDF is

$$\tilde{S}_t = S_t \frac{\tilde{H}_0}{\tilde{H}_t} = \exp(\tilde{\eta} t) \frac{\tilde{e}(X_0)}{\tilde{e}(X_t)}$$

There are two problems with the recovery procedure:

  1. The candidate solution for $\tilde{e}(x)$ may not be unique – addressed with ergodicity restriction
  2. Even if we do have a unique $\tilde{e}(x)$, the procedure may will not recover the true probability distribution unless we also impose functional form restriction on SDF.
