Notes on Risk price Dynamics @Borovicka:2011kw

Section 2 Markov Pricing with Brownian Information

State follows a standard diffusion:

$$d X_t = \mu(X_t) + \sigma(X_t) dW_t$$

Functionals

Additive functionals have the form

$$ A = \int_0^t \beta(X_u) du + \int_0^t \alpha(X_u) \cdot dW_u $$

where $\alpha$ and $\beta$ may be non-linear functions of the state $X$.

Multiplicative functions are $M_t = \exp(A_t)$ for some additive functional $A_t$ parameterized by $(\beta, \alpha)$.

Two types of multiplicative functionals in paper:

  1. Stochastic growth functional ($G$) parameterized by $(\beta_G, \alpha_G)$: captures evolution of cash flows or other macro quantities
  2. Stochastic discount functionals ($S$) parameterized by $(\beta_S, \alpha_S)$: represents marginal valuation and typically decays exponentially

Perturbations to multiplicative functionals are $M H®$, where $H$ is a multiplicative functional characterized by $(\beta_h(x, r), r \alpha_d(x))$, with $\beta(x,0)=0 \; \forall x$ and $\alpha_d(x)$ defines the direction of the risk exposure.

Denote $\beta_d(x) := \frac{d}{dr} \betah(x, r) \bigm\vert{r=0}$.

Let $D$ be the additive functional characterized by $(\beta_d, \alpha_d)$.

Risk Elasticities

Risk elasticities are given by

$$ \frac{d}{dr} \log E [M_t H_t® | X0 = x] \bigm\vert{r=0} = \frac{E[M_t D_t | X_0 =x]}{E[M_t | X_0=x]}$$

We can represent $M$ as a stochastic integral:

$$M_t = \int0^t \chi{u,t} \cdot dW_u + E[M_t | X_0 =x],$$

where

$$\chi_{u,t} = E[M_t|M_u, X_u] [\psi(X_u,t-u) + \alpha(X_u)]$$

$$\psi(x,v) = \sigma(x)’ \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \log E[M_v | X_0=x] \right).$$

Result 2.1 is that we can write the risk elasticity as

$$\frac{1}{t} \frac{d}{dr} \log E [M_t H_t® | X0 = x] \bigm\vert{r=0} = \frac{1}{t} \frac{E \left[ M_t \int_0^t \epsilon(X_u, t-u) du | X_0=x \right]}{E [M_t | X_0=x]}$$

where

$$\epsilon(x, v) \equiv \alpha_d(x) \cdot [\psi(x,v) + \alpha(x)] + \beta_d(x)$$

is a shock elasticity function.

Martingale Decomposition

We can factor $M$ as

$$M_t = \exp(\eta t) \hat{M}_t \frac{e(X_0)}{e(X_t)},$$

where $e$ and $\eta$ solve

$$E[M_t e(X_t) | X_0 =x] = \exp(\eta t) e(x)$$

for any $t$ where $e$ is strictly positive. This problem can be localized by computing

$$\lim_{t \rightarrow 0} \frac{E[M_t e(X_t) | X_0=x] - \exp(\eta t) e(x)}{t} = 0.$$

The local counter part is

$$\mathbb{B} e = \eta e.$$

For a diffusion model and smooth $f$ we have

$$\mathbb{B} f = \left(\beta + \frac{1}{2} |\alpha|^2 \right)f + (\sigma \alpha+ \mu)\cdot \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} + \frac{1}{2} \text{trace} \left(\sigma \sigma’ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial x’} \right)$$

NOTE: if we parameterize $M$ by $(\beta, \alpha)$, then Girsanov’s Theorem says

$$dW_t = [\alpha(X_t) + \nu(X_t)]dt + d\hat{W}_t,$$

where

$$Î½= \sigma’ \left[\frac{\partial \log e}{\partial x} \right]$$

NOTE: In risk-price dynamics $nu(x) = alpha_e(x)$. they are the exposure of $e(x)$ to $dW_t$

Elasticity under new measure

Under the measure implied by the martingale $\hat{M}$ we have $\hat{\beta}_d = \beta_d + \alpha_d \cdot(\alpha + \nu)$. Let $\hat{E}$ be the expectation operator under the new measure and let $\hat{e} = \frac{1}{e}$. Then we can write our risk elasticity as

$$ \frac{d}{dr} \log E [M_t H_t® | X0 = x] \bigm\vert{r=0} =\frac{\hat{E} \left[ \hat{e}(X_t) \int_0^t \epsilon(X_u, t-u) du | X_0=x \right]}{E [\hat{e}(X_t) | X_0=x]}.$$

Under the new measure we can write

$$\psi(x,v) = \sigma(x)’ \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\log E[M_v|X_0=x] \right) = \phi(x,v) - \phi(x,0),$$

where

$$\phi(x,v) = \sigma(x)’ \left(\frac{\partial}{\partial x} \log \hat{E} [\hat{e}(X_v) | X_0=x\right).$$

Result 2.2 then gives another way to write the risk elasticity in terms of the solution to the eigenfunction problem:

$$ \frac{1}{t} \frac{d}{dr} \log E [M_t H_t® | X0 = x] \bigm\vert{r=0} = \frac{1}{r} \frac{\hat{E} \left[ \hat{e}(X_t) \int_0^t \epsilon(X_u, t-u) du | X_0=x \right]}{E [\hat{e}(X_t) | X_0=x]},$$

where

$$\epsilon(x, v) \equiv \alpha_d(x) \cdot \left[\phi(x,v) + \nu(x) + \alpha(x) \right] + \beta_d(x).$$

This result lets us obtain an integral representation for risk elasticities.

NOTE: we have $\nu(x) = - \phi(x,0)$ and $\nu$ captures how the dominant eigenfunction $e$ is exposed to shocks.1

Section 3: Price and exposure elasticities

Let $G$ be a cash flow and $S$ be a stochastic discount factor.

The expected return of $G$ over an investment horizon $t$ subject to a perturbation $H$ is

$$\frac{E[G_t H_t®||X_0=x]}{E[S_t G_t H_t® | X_0=x]}.$$

This can easily be manipulated to get the risk price elasticity: (logs, derivative wrt r, scale by 1/t)

$$\pi(x, t) = \frac{1}{t} \frac{d}{dr} \log E[G_t H_t® |X0=x] \bigm\vert{r=0} - \frac{1}{t} \frac{d}{dr} \log E[S_t G_t H_t® |X0=x] \bigm\vert{r=0}.$$

This is composed of two pieces:

  1. Risk exposure elasticity: captures how $G$ changes when perturbed by $H®$ (positive term)
  2. Risk-value elasticity: Captures how the value changes as exposure to risk changes (negative term).

Martingale Perturbations

If we choose $H$ to be a (local) martingale, then its drift component will not distort the volatilities under the change in measure. This means that cash flows and other multiplicative functionals will not be distorted by choosing such a perturbation. To impose this condition we must have

$$\beta_h(x, r) = - \frac{1}{2} | \alpha_d(x)|^2.$$

In this case we have $\beta_d=0$ and $\hat{\beta}_d = \alpha_d \cdot (\alpha + \nu)$, which means

$$D_t = \int_0^t \alpha_d(X_u) \cdot dW_u.$$

With martingale perturbations, the shock elasticities are impulse responses. See section 3.2 for more information on how this works.

We can then use result 2.2 and the expression for the risk-price elasticity to get an integral representation of the risk-price elasticity. With a martingale perturbation we get more simple forms for the elasticities with respect to $G$ and $SG$, but the changes of measure with respect to $M=G$ and $M=SG$ will almost certainly be different. This makes things complicated.

We could instead, however, impose that $G$ is local martingale. This means $E[G_t|X_0=x]=1$ always. Furthermore, if we have that $GH$ is a martingale, then $E[G_t H_t® } X_0=x]=1$ for all $t$ and $r$. This makes the risk-exposure elasticity drop out of the risk-price elasticity because $\log(E[GH]) = \log(1) = 0$.
