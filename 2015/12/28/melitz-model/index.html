These are some of my notes to summplement what can be found in Kim Ruhl’s notes here

Notation

Below is a table of notation. WLOG a subscript $i$ means for country $i$. Also, sometimes there are variants $x_{ij}$ and $x_i$ of variables. Here we only list the $x_{ij}$ variant and it is to be understood that the version without the $j$ is an aggregate for country $i$ across all other countries $j$ or simply shorthand to ease notation – which use is clear by context. Model parameters are not included in the table.

Demand for Varieties

To derive equation (3) (CES demand for goods), I follow these steps:

  1. Take FOC wrt $c_{0i}$ and $c_i(\omega)$.
  2. Use the FOC wrt $c_i(\omega)$ for a good $\omega_1$ and a generic good $\omega$ and take the ratio
  3. Do some algebra to put $p_i(\omega) c_i(\omega)$ alone on the RHS. This will require multiplying by 1 and moving some $p_i(\omega)$ terms to the LHS.
  4. Integrate both sides over all $\omega$.
  5. Use budget constraint to eliminate the $\int p_i(\omega) c_i(\omega) d \omega$ on the RHS.
  6. Re-arrange to get something that looks like equation 3.

At the time of writing (2015-12-28 15:09), Applying this set of steps gave me an expression that differs from Kim’s in two ways:

  1. He has a leading $\alpha$, where mine cancelled out in step 2
  2. He doesn’t have a $-p_{0i} c_{0i}$ anywhwere and I do (it came in step 5).

Firm problem

The firm problem is pretty straightforward. Just remember that the firm takes demand (equation 3) as given, which means we plug it in to the objective.

Also, we can read labor directly off the constraint.

Notice a few things about the prices: - They feature a constant markup over marginal pricing - Prices don't differ across markets, except to compensate for vairable trade costs. This means that firms don't price discriminate across markets (*In the data they do*) - Prices depend only on the productivity of the firm producing a good, not by the name of the good (not directly a function of $\omega$) - Profits are a constant fraction of total revenues (driven by CES demand and monopolistic competition)

Equilibrium

In equilibrium, for each $i,j$ country pair a firm from country $i$ with cutoff productivity $\hat{\varphi}_{ij}$ is indifferent to selling in market $j$. This is characterized by the setting $\pi_{ij}(\hat{\varphi}_{ij}) = 0$ and solving for $\hat{\varphi}_{ij}$.

This means that not all firms export and that larger/more productive firms are the exporters. This is found in the data and is driven by the modeling assumption of fixed export costs between $i$ and $j$.

In this world we end up with a linear system of equations in terms of per country total profits. We get existence of equilibrium from the existence of a solution to this system (work comes through Perron Frobeneius theory).

Trade flows from $i$ to $j$ (value of goods $i$ exports to $j$: $p_{ij} c_{ij}$) in this model are simply a share of total expenditure (or total profit – in eq. they are the same with no assets) in country $j$.

Studying the intensive margin reveals that trade flows move with parameter governing degree of heterogeneity, not elasticity of substitution – driven by assumed distribution of productivity.

Elasticity of trade wrt variable trade costs is higher in this model than in one without heterogeneity in productivity.

Elasticity of trade wrt fixed trade costs is higher when elasticity of consumption is low (extensive margin – low elasticity => low substitutibility => high opportunity for profit).

Chaney’s financial structure

Chaney 2008 assumes that $Y_i = w_i L_i (1 + \pi)$, where $\pi$ is the return on a global mutual fund. In our setup we had $Y_i = w_i L_i + \pi_i$ (from assumption that agents in country $i$ own firms in country $i$.)

It is kinda remarkable that Chaney’s structure ends up giving the same country level profits as the model here, even with asymmetric trade costs.
